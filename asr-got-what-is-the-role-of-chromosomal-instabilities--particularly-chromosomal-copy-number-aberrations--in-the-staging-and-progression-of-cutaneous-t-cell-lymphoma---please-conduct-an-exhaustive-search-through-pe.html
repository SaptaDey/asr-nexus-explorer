## Executive Summary ### **Executive Summary** Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL) presents a significant clinical challenge due to its highly variable disease course, where patients with similar clinical stages can experience vastly different outcomes. The current TNMB (Tumor-Node-Metastasis-Blood) staging system, while clinically essential, is insufficient for accurately predicting which patients with early-stage disease will remain indolent versus those who will rapidly progress to aggressive, life-threatening forms. This research addresses the critical need for robust molecular biomarkers by conducting an exhaustive, data-centric investigation into the role of chromosomal instability, particularly copy number aberrations (CNAs), in the staging and progression of CTCL. The central finding of this comprehensive literature analysis is that the accumulation of CNAs is not a stochastic epiphenomenon but a core driver of CTCL progression and a powerful, independent prognostic factor. The genomic landscape of CTCL evolves from a relatively "quiet" state in early-stage Mycosis Fungoides (MF) to a state of profound chromosomal chaos in advanced tumor-stage MF and its leukemic variant, Sézary Syndrome (SS). This is quantitatively demonstrated by a direct correlation between increasing CNA burden—the fraction of the genome altered—and advancing disease stage. Several specific, recurrent CNAs have been identified as high-impact prognostic markers with direct correlations to mortality. The most critical of these is the **loss of the 9p21.3 locus**, which contains the *CDKN2A/B* tumor suppressor genes. This aberration is strongly associated with large-cell transformation, disease progression, and significantly shorter overall survival, establishing it as a key biomarker of aggressive disease. Other prognostically significant aberrations consistently identified across multiple high-impact studies include gains of oncogenic loci such as **17q** (containing *STAT3/5*) and **8q24** (*MYC*), and losses of tumor suppressor regions like **10q** (*PTEN*) and **17p** (*TP53*). A primary objective of this research was to move beyond summarizing published findings and to locate actionable, downloadable datasets for independent computational analysis. This objective was successfully met. The investigation identified several key studies, most notably **Park et al. (2021, *Blood*)**, **Wang et al.
